Wolf Species

By Jinhee Han
Written 2020, November 28

There are three main species of wolves that are recognized today - the gray wolf, red wolf, and Eastern wolf. These species are further divided into various subspecies depending on the area they occupy. For example, gray Wolves that have adapted to living in cold environments are called artic wolves. All three species share similarities as they share a common ancestor. However, evolution has contributed to substantial differences. In this article, we will be covering information unique to each species.

Species of Wolves

Gray Wolf

Gray wolves (Canis lupus), also known as the timber wolf, are the most common species of wolves. Some subspecies include the artic wolf (Canis lupus arctos), northwestern wolf (Canis lupus occidentalis ), great plains wolf (Canis lupus nubilus), and mexican wolf (Canis lupus baileyi). While originating in North America, gray wolves can be found in Australia, Europe, Asia, Middle America, as well as North America. They live in forests, wetlands, deserts, and rocky areas. A typical adult is 4-6 feet (120-200 cm) long and weighs 40-175 pounds (18-79 kg). As its names suggests, gray wolves usually have gray fur. However, pure white and black variations exist.

Red Wolf

The red wolf (Canis rufus) is currently the most endangered wolf species. They are only found in Eastern North Carolina due to predator control programs from the early 20th century. Red wolves are mostly brown with black along their blacks and reddish fur located on their ears, head, and legs. The average adult weighs 40-80 pounds (18-36 kg).It is 4 feet long (120 cm) and around 25 inches (63 cm) wide, making it slightly smaller than the average gray wolf.

Eastern Wolf

The Eastern Wolf (Canis lycaon) was originally categorized as a subspecies of the gray wolf. However, new data lead scientists to believe that eastern wolves are an entirely different species. Eastern wolves are only found in North America.
